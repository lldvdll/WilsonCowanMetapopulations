Background

The Biology: Why Do We Model Brain Networks?

▶

Your brain has about 86 billion neurons. They don't work alone - they form networks. Groups of neurons in different brain regions talk to each other by sending electrical and chemical signals. These signals don't arrive instantly; there's a delay (like lag in a video call) because signals have to physically travel along nerve fibers.

Scientists want to understand how these networks produce the brain's rhythmic activity (brain waves like alpha, beta, gamma). Instead of simulating every single neuron (impossibly expensive), they use mean-field population models that describe the average behaviour of thousands of neurons at once. "Mean-field" just means "replace the effect of many individual neurons with a single average." That's what Wilson-Cowan models and their successors do.

Neural population models describe the collective dynamics of large ensembles of neurons using a small number of state variables (e.g., mean firing rate, mean membrane potential). These are mean-field models: instead of tracking every individual neuron, they describe the average (mean) behaviour of the population. The term "mean-field" comes from statistical physics, where the effect of all other particles on one particle is approximated by a single average "field." In neuroscience, the mean field is the average synaptic input received by a typical neuron in the population. The Wilson-Cowan model (1972) was among the first such mean-field model, capturing the interaction between excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I) populations via a system of coupled ODEs with sigmoidal nonlinearities.

Brain connectivity can be described at three levels: structural connectivity (axonal projections), functional connectivity (statistical correlations between region activities), and effective connectivity (causal influence). Cortical hubs, scale-free properties, and small-world architecture are observed in empirical connectomics. Time delays arise from finite axonal conduction velocities (typically 1-12 m/s) and are crucial determinants of network dynamics.

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019), Introduction, pp. 1-2 (three levels of brain connectivity, cortical hubs); Forrester et al. (2024), Introduction, pp. 1-3 (functional connectivity, neural mass modelling history); Notes, Section 8.3, p. 343 (population firing rate framework)

Excitatory and Inhibitory Neurons

▶

Think of excitatory neurons as "accelerator" neurons — they make other neurons more likely to fire. Inhibitory neurons are "brake" neurons — they calm things down. A healthy brain needs a balance. Too much excitation = seizures. Too much inhibition = coma. The interplay between E and I creates the rhythmic oscillations we see in EEG recordings.

Approximately 80% of cortical neurons are excitatory and 20% are inhibitory. This ~4:1 ratio is a key constraint that any realistic brain model must respect. The Wilson-Cowan model and the next generation model both describe the dynamics of these two populations.

Approximately 80% of cortical neurons are excitatory (glutamatergic, primarily pyramidal cells) and 20% are inhibitory (GABAergic interneurons). Excitatory connections increase postsynaptic membrane potential toward firing threshold, while inhibitory connections hyperpolarize target neurons. The balance of E/I interactions gives rise to population-level oscillatory dynamics. The Wilson-Cowan framework captures this E-I balance through coupled rate equations with nonlinear (sigmoidal) activation functions, representing the fraction of neurons in each population that are active at time t.

References: Notes, Section 8.3, pp. 343-344 (excitatory/inhibitory populations, sigmoid firing rate Eq. 8.4); Conti & Van Gorder (2019), p. 2, Eqs. 1-2 (E-I metapopulation formulation)

Brain Oscillation Frequency Bands

▶

Brain waves are rhythmic electrical patterns measured by EEG/MEG. They come in distinct frequency bands, each associated with different brain states:

  • Delta (1-4 Hz): Deep sleep, unconsciousness
  • Theta (4-8 Hz): Drowsiness, memory formation (hippocampus)
  • Alpha (8-13 Hz): Relaxed wakefulness, eyes closed
  • Beta (13-30 Hz): Active thinking, motor planning, concentration
  • Gamma (30-100+ Hz): Attention, perception, consciousness, binding of information

The Wilson-Cowan model at ~50 Hz produces gamma-band oscillations. The next generation model can produce oscillations across multiple frequency bands simultaneously, which is closer to how real brains work.

Neural oscillations arise from the interplay of excitatory and inhibitory populations and are characterized by power spectral density peaks in specific frequency bands. The WC model with default parameters (Conti & Van Gorder: β=10, τ1=1, τ2=1.4) produces oscillations at approximately 50 Hz (gamma band). The next generation NMM, by incorporating within-population synchrony dynamics via the Kuramoto order parameter Z, can exhibit amplitude modulation and multi-band dynamics. Forrester et al. (2024) explicitly analyze functional connectivity across 8 frequency bands from delta to high gamma, demonstrating that the model captures frequency-dependent FC patterns matching empirical MEG data.

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019), p. 4 (oscillation frequency ~50 Hz with default parameters); Forrester et al. (2024), Fig. 2, p. 8 (8 frequency bands: δ 1-4 Hz, θ 4-8 Hz, α 8-15 Hz, low-β 15-26 Hz, high-β 26-35 Hz, low-γ 25-50 Hz, mid-γ 50-76 Hz, high-γ 76-120 Hz)

Dale's Principle and E-I Architecture

▶

Dale's Principle states that a neuron releases the same neurotransmitter(s) at all of its synapses. In practice, this means each neuron is either excitatory OR inhibitory — never both. This is why the Wilson-Cowan model separates neurons into E and I populations.

Another key biological fact: long-range connections between brain regions are almost exclusively excitatory (glutamatergic pyramidal neurons). Inhibitory neurons (GABAergic interneurons) mostly act locally within a region. This is why both Conti & Van Gorder and Forrester et al. only couple excitatory populations between nodes — it's not a simplification, it's biology.

Dale's principle (strictly, Dale's law as commonly interpreted) constrains network architecture: each neuron releases only one type of neurotransmitter, so pyramidal cells are exclusively excitatory and interneurons exclusively inhibitory. Cortico-cortical projections are predominantly formed by pyramidal cell axons and are therefore excitatory, justifying the excitatory-only inter-node coupling in both models (Eq. 1 of Conti: only Ej terms appear in the inter-node sum; Eq. 10 of Forrester: only REj drives inter-node coupling). Dale's principle is a fundamental constraint that any biologically plausible neural network model must respect.

Levels of Brain Connectivity

▶

Neuroscientists describe brain connectivity at three distinct levels:

  • Structural connectivity (SC): The physical wiring — which brain regions are connected by nerve fibers (axons). Measured using diffusion MRI tractography. This is the "road map."
  • Functional connectivity (FC): Statistical correlations between the activities of different brain regions. Measured from EEG, MEG, or fMRI time series. This is the "traffic pattern" — which regions tend to be active at the same time.
  • Effective connectivity (EC): The causal influence one brain region exerts on another. Harder to measure; requires interventions or sophisticated modelling (e.g., Dynamic Causal Modelling).

Your project operates at the structural connectivity level (the adjacency matrix W), but you will also compute functional connectivity from the model output using Pearson correlation and PLV (see Step 4 in the Implementation Guide). This lets you study the structure-function relationship: how does the physical wiring (SC) shape the patterns of co-activity (FC)?

The three levels of connectivity are distinct but related: structural connectivity constrains but does not fully determine functional connectivity. SC provides the substrate over which dynamics unfold, and FC emerges from the interaction of SC with intrinsic node dynamics and time delays. Forrester et al. (2024) explicitly model this relationship: SC from HCP diffusion MRI is the input, and model-generated FC (computed from simulated time series via cross-spectral analysis) is compared with empirical MEG FC. The key finding is that structure alone (eigenmode analysis) has limited predictive power for FC; dynamics must be included.

For your project: The adjacency matrix W encodes SC. Conti & Van Gorder use idealised topologies (path, cycle, complete, lattice) as abstract SC. You study how SC topology affects dynamics. Computing model FC (e.g., pairwise Pearson correlation of RE(t) time series across nodes, and PLV from the Kuramoto order parameter phases) bridges structure and function — a key analysis for comparing topologies and delay strategies.

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 1-2 (three levels of connectivity); Forrester et al. (2024), pp. 8-10 (structural connectivity from HCP, Desikan-Killiany atlas, 68 regions); Forrester et al. (2024), pp. 15-18 (SC eigenmode analysis vs. dynamic FC prediction)

The E-I Balance Hypothesis

▶

Healthy brain function requires a delicate balance between excitation and inhibition. When this balance is disrupted:

  • Too much excitation → Epileptic seizures (hypersynchronous firing)
  • Too much inhibition → Coma-like states, cognitive impairment
  • Subtle imbalance → Implicated in autism spectrum disorders, schizophrenia, ADHD

The Wilson-Cowan model captures this balance through the interplay of WEE (self-excitation) and WEI (inhibitory brake). The Hopf bifurcation in the model corresponds to the tipping point where the E-I balance shifts from stable equilibrium to oscillation. The next generation model adds another dimension: within-population synchrony (|Z|), which provides finer insight into how E-I balance affects coherent network activity.

The E-I balance hypothesis posits that cortical circuits operate in a regime where excitatory and inhibitory synaptic currents approximately cancel, producing fluctuation-driven dynamics. In the WC model, the Inhibition-Stabilised Network (ISN) regime (Notes, p. 347) captures this: strong WEE is stabilised by strong I feedback, producing damped gamma oscillations and a resonant frequency response. Disruption of this balance (e.g., by modifying WEE/WEI ratio) can push the system through a Hopf bifurcation (Eq. 8.8) into sustained oscillations or, conversely, into a pathological fixed point. Conti & Van Gorder (2019) relate network topology effects to neurodegeneration: loss of connectivity (as in Alzheimer's) disrupts the E-I balance across the network, potentially explaining the transition from healthy oscillatory dynamics to pathological regimes.

References: Notes, p. 347 (ISN regime, E-I balance, gamma oscillations); Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 10-11 (neurodegeneration implications, loss of connectivity disrupting dynamics)

Chemical vs. Electrical Synapses

▶

Chemical synapses are like sending a letter: neuron A releases chemical messengers (neurotransmitters) that float across a gap and bind to receptors on neuron B. This takes time (1-100 ms) and the signal can only go one direction.

Electrical synapses (gap junctions) are like a direct wire between two neurons. Current flows directly through tiny channels connecting them. It's much faster (~0.2 ms) and can go both ways. They're especially common between inhibitory neurons.

The original Wilson-Cowan model only includes chemical synapses. The next generation model adds gap junctions too, which turns out to be important for realistic brain dynamics.

Chemical synapses involve neurotransmitter release triggered by presynaptic action potentials, producing a conductance change in the postsynaptic neuron modelled as κsg(t)(vsyn - v(t)), where g(t) represents a time-dependent conductance and vsyn is the reversal potential. The synaptic time course is often modelled with an alpha function: g(t) = α2t exp(-αt)H(t).

Electrical synapses (gap junctions) are modelled as ohmic connections: Igap = κv(vp - vn), where κv is the gap junction coupling strength. These are bidirectional, voltage-dependent, and instantaneous. They are predominantly found between interneurons and are thought to promote synchrony within inhibitory populations.

References: Forrester et al. (2024), pp. 5-7, Eqs. 7-9 (chemical synaptic conductance model, gap junction terms κvab); Notes, Section 8.9, pp. 354-356, Eqs. 8.84-8.85 (synaptic conductance in R,V formulation); Forrester et al. (2024), p. 6 & Table in S1 (gap junctions predominantly I-I, with κvEI = κvIE = 0 by default)

Time Delays in the Brain

▶

When one brain region sends a signal to another, it doesn't arrive instantly. Signals travel along nerve fibers (axons) at finite speeds. The delay depends on:

  • Distance: Far-apart regions have longer delays
  • Myelination: Myelinated axons conduct faster (up to 120 m/s) than unmyelinated ones (0.5-2 m/s)
  • Synaptic processing: Chemical transmission itself takes time

Within a single brain region, delays are shorter (intra-node delays). Between regions, delays are longer (inter-node delays). These delays fundamentally change the mathematics: instead of ordinary differential equations (ODEs), we need delay differential equations (DDEs), which are harder to solve because the future depends not just on the present state but also on past states.

Conduction delays Tij = dij/v arise from finite axonal conduction velocities v (typically 6-12 m/s for myelinated cortical axons). In the original paper by Conti & Van Gorder, two categories of delays are distinguished:

  • Intra-node delays (τ1, τ2): representing local processing time within a cortical area between E and I subpopulations
  • Inter-node delays (ρnj): representing axonal conduction time between cortical areas n and j

The inclusion of delays transforms the system from ODEs to DDEs, y'(t) = f(t, y(t), y(t-τ1), ..., y(t-τk)), which require specification of a history function for t ≤ t0 and specialized solvers (e.g., MATLAB's DDE23, Python's jitcdde). Delays can promote oscillatory behavior, cause instabilities through Hopf bifurcations, and introduce complex spatiotemporal patterns including travelling waves.

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 2-3, Eqs. 1-2 (intra-node delays τ1, τ2 and inter-node delays ρnj); Forrester et al. (2024), p. 8, Eq. 10 (distance-dependent delays Tij = dij/v); Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 6-9, Figs. 6-8 (effect of delays on dynamics)
Model

The Original Wilson-Cowan Model (1972)

▶

Imagine you have two groups of people at a party: the "excited" group (E) who are dancing and making noise, and the "calm" group (I) who are trying to quiet things down. The excited group gets more excited when they hear noise (self-excitation), but the calm group tries to dampen them. Meanwhile, the excited group also stirs up the calm group. This push-and-pull creates oscillations - the party gets loud, then quiet, then loud again.

The Wilson-Cowan model captures this with just two variables and two equations. It's beautifully simple and was revolutionary when introduced in 1972. But it has limitations: it doesn't tell you anything about individual neurons, it was designed for a single brain region (no network), and it uses a somewhat arbitrary sigmoid function rather than being derived from actual neuron physics.

The Wilson-Cowan model (1972) is a mean-field rate model describing the time evolution of the proportions of excitatory and inhibitory neurons active per unit time. It is phenomenological, meaning the sigmoidal activation function f(x) = 1/(1+exp(-βx)) is chosen as a physiologically reasonable approximation rather than derived from single-neuron biophysics.

References: Notes, Section 8.3, pp. 343-347 (full derivation and analysis); Wilson & Cowan (1972) original formulation; Notes, Eq. 8.4 (general sigmoid with threshold g0 and saturation f0), Eq. 8.5 (model equations). Note: The simplified sigmoid f(x) = 1/(1+e-βx) shown here is the specific choice used for bifurcation analysis on p. 344 (and by Conti & Van Gorder); the general form (Eq. 8.4) has additional parameters f0, g0, r.
Wilson-Cowan Equations (Eq. 8.5 from Notes)
τE dE/dt = -E + (1 - rEE) · fE[WEEE - WEII + pE]
τI dI/dt = -I + (1 - rII) · fI[WIEE - WIII + pI]

What Can This Model Do?

▶

Despite being simple (just 2 equations), the Wilson-Cowan model can produce:

  • Stable rest: both E and I settle at constant values (fixed point)
  • Oscillations: rhythmic alternation between high and low activity (limit cycle) - this is like brain waves!
  • Bistability: two possible resting states - the brain can switch between them (like "up" and "down" states seen in sleep)

You can study these behaviors by drawing nullclines (curves where dE/dt=0 and dI/dt=0) in the E-I phase plane. Where they intersect gives you the fixed points.

What is a bifurcation? A bifurcation is a critical point where a small change in a parameter causes a sudden qualitative change in the system's behaviour. Think of it like a tipping point. The Hopf bifurcation is the most important one here: it's the moment where a stable resting state becomes unstable and oscillations spontaneously appear. In the model, you can trigger this by increasing WEE (self-excitation) past a critical value — try it in the interactive plot below!

The 2D system supports a rich bifurcation structure. For fa(x) = 1/(1+e-βx), the fixed point (Ē, Ī) satisfies the nonlinear system (Eq. 8.6). The Jacobian matrix (Eq. 8.7) yields conditions for:

  • Hopf bifurcation: Tr(J) = 0 with det(J) > 0, leading to stable limit cycles (Eq. 8.8)
  • Saddle-node bifurcation: det(J) = 0, creating or destroying fixed points
  • Bogdanov-Takens bifurcation: Tr(J) = det(J) = 0 (codimension-2)
  • SNIC bifurcation: Saddle-node on an invariant circle

The Inhibition-Stabilised Network (ISN) regime, with strong E-E excitation balanced by strong I feedback, produces damped gamma-frequency oscillations and exhibits a resonant frequency response to periodic forcing.

References: Notes, Section 8.3, pp. 344-346: fixed point analysis (Eq. 8.6), Jacobian (Eq. 8.7), Hopf bifurcation condition (Eq. 8.8), nullcline analysis (Fig. 8.1); Notes, p. 347 (ISN regime, damped gamma oscillations); Notes, pp. 347-349 (piecewise linear variant with Heaviside function)

Original Paper

Conti & Van Gorder (2019): Network Wilson-Cowan with Delays

▶

This is the paper your project is based on. The authors took the Wilson-Cowan model and asked: "What happens if we connect many of these E-I units into a network, AND add realistic time delays?" They placed Wilson-Cowan oscillators on different types of graphs (networks) and studied how the shape of the network and the size of the delays affected the brain-like rhythms.

Conti & Van Gorder (2019) extend the Wilson-Cowan framework to a metapopulation model incorporating both intra-node delays (within cortical regions) and inter-node delays (between regions). This produces a system of 2N coupled delay differential equations on a graph, where the adjacency matrix W encodes the network topology. The paper is novel in combining both types of delays on generic networks.

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 1-3 (introduction and model formulation); Eqs. 1-2 (full DDE system); Table 1, p. 4 (default parameter values)

The Model Equations

▶
Conti & Van Gorder Model (Eqs. 1-2)
(1/Te) dEn/dt = -En + Se(ceeEn(t-τ1,n) + cieIn(t-τ2,n) + Pn + kn ∑j Wnj Ej(t-ρnj))

(1/(Tiαn)) dIn/dt = -In + Si(ceiEn(t-τ2,n) + ciiIn(t-τ1,n) + Qn)

For each brain region n (node in the network):

  • The E population at node n is driven by its own past E activity (delayed by τ1), its own past I activity (delayed by τ2), external input P, and signals from E populations at other connected nodes (delayed by ρnj)
  • The I population is only driven locally - it receives input from E and I within its own node (both delayed), plus external input Q
  • Crucially, only excitatory populations send signals between nodes (this is biologically accurate - long-range cortical connections are primarily excitatory)

The model generalizes several existing formulations. Setting kn=0 recovers the single-node delay model of Coombes & Laing (2009). Setting all delays to zero and kn=0 recovers the original Wilson-Cowan (1972). The parameter 1/αn introduces a relative timescale between E and I populations. The sigmoid function S(x) = 1/(1+exp(-βx)) maps input to activity with steepness parameter β. The system has 2N state variables and depends on the history over the interval [t - max(τ1, τ2, max ρnj), t].

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 2-3, Eqs. 1-2 (model equations); p. 3 (reduction to Coombes & Laing 2009 and original WC as special cases); Table 1, p. 4 (default parameters: cee=cei=1, cie=cii=-1, β=10, α=0.6, P=Q=0.5, τ1=1, τ2=1.4, ρ=10)

Network Topologies Studied

▶

The authors tested four different shapes of networks (N=16 and N=100 nodes):

  • Path graph: Nodes in a line (1-2-3-...-N). Each node connects to its neighbors only. Very sparse.
  • Complete graph: Every node connects to every other node. Maximally dense.
  • Cycle graph: Nodes in a ring. Like a path graph but the ends connect. Still sparse.
  • Square lattice: Nodes on a grid, each connecting to 4 neighbors. Intermediate density.

The key question: does the shape of the network matter? Answer: YES, very much.

Key Findings

▶
Main result: Network topology can regularize or deregularize dynamics. Dense, homogeneous networks (complete graph) produce stable oscillations. Sparse, heterogeneous networks (path graph, lattice) are more susceptible to chaotic or desynchronized behavior.

Think of it this way: if everyone in a group can communicate with everyone else (complete graph), they can stay in sync. If communication only passes along a chain (path graph), it's easy for the message to get distorted. Specific findings:

  • Coupling strength: Stronger connections between nodes stabilize the network (nodes coordinate better)
  • Inter-node delays: Larger delays between nodes decrease the oscillation frequency but have a regularizing effect
  • Intra-node delays: These tend to break synchrony, unless all nodes have the same delay values
  • Network degree: Higher degree (more connections per node) = more stable. The complete graph is the most stable.
  • Chaos regularization: Chaotic dynamics on path/lattice graphs can be regularized by the complete graph topology

The paper demonstrates that the mean degree of the network is a primary determinant of dynamical stability. Bifurcation diagrams (Fig. 7) show oscillation frequency as a function of intra- and inter-node delays, revealing critical thresholds τcrit and ρcrit beyond which biologically relevant oscillation frequencies (30-90 Hz) are obtained. The regularization of chaotic dynamics (Fig. 8, using the parameter set from Coombes & Laing 2009) by increasing network connectivity has implications for understanding neurodegeneration, where loss of connectivity may promote pathological dynamics.

References: Conti & Van Gorder (2019): Fig. 3, p. 5 (single-node dynamics, limit cycles); Figs. 4-5, pp. 5-6 (coupling strength effects on N=16 networks); Fig. 6, p. 7 (topology comparison, N=16); Fig. 7, pp. 7-8 (oscillation frequency vs. delay bifurcation diagrams); Fig. 8, p. 9 (chaos regularization on path vs. complete graph); Fig. 9, pp. 9-10 (N=100 network results); pp. 10-11 (discussion on neurodegeneration implications)

Limitations (What Your Project Will Address)

▶
  • All delays are the same: Every connection between nodes has the same delay ρ. In reality, delays vary with distance.
  • Wilson-Cowan is phenomenological: The sigmoid activation isn't derived from real neuron physics. Parameters don't have direct biophysical meaning.
  • No synchrony measure: The model can't tell you whether neurons within a population are firing in sync or randomly.
  • No gap junctions: Only chemical synapses are modeled.
  • Only 4 simple topologies: Real brain networks are much more complex (small-world, modular, etc.).

Your supervisor's suggestions directly address all of these: use a more modern model (fixes 2,3,4), add varying delays (fixes 1), and study different topologies (fixes 5).

Recommended Paper

Forrester et al. (2024): Next Gen NMM with Gap Junctions

▶

This is the paper your supervisor recommended. It introduces a much more sophisticated brain model that:

  • Is mathematically derived from actual neuron physics (not just a plausible guess)
  • Includes both chemical and electrical synapses
  • Can track how synchronized neurons are within each population
  • Uses real brain connectivity data (Human Connectome Project)
  • Produces dynamics that match real brain recordings (MEG) much better than older models

The key idea: start with a network of spiking neurons, then use a mathematical trick (Ott-Antonsen reduction) to get exact equations for the average behavior. You get the best of both worlds - biophysical accuracy AND computational tractability.

Forrester et al. (2024) employ a next generation neural mass model derived from an exact mean-field reduction of a network of quadratic integrate-and-fire (QIF) neurons with both chemical and electrical (gap junction) synaptic interactions. The model is embedded in a 68-node whole-brain network using structural connectivity and path-length data from the Human Connectome Project. The paper demonstrates that this biophysically-grounded model, particularly with the inclusion of gap junctions, produces functional connectivity patterns that more closely match empirical MEG and BOLD fMRI data than phenomenological alternatives (e.g., Jansen-Rit).

References: Forrester et al. (2024), pp. 1-4 (introduction, motivation for next gen NMM over Jansen-Rit); pp. 4-8 (model formulation, Eqs. 1-10); pp. 8-10 (structural connectivity from HCP, 68-region Desikan-Killiany atlas); pp. 19-22 (results: FC comparison with empirical MEG data, Figs. 7-10)

The Model Equations (What You'll Implement)

▶
Next Generation Neural Mass Model - Single Node (Eqs. 7-9, Forrester et al.)
Firing rate:
τa dRa/dt = -Ra ∑b(gab + κvab) + 2RaVa + Δa/(πτa)

Mean membrane potential:
τa dVa/dt = η0a + Va2 - π2τa2Ra2 + ∑bgab(vsynab - Va) + ∑bκvab(Vb - Va)

Synaptic dynamics:
dgab/dt = αab(sab - gab)
dsab/dt = αab(κsabRb - sab)

where a, b ∈ {E, I} → 12 ODEs per node

Don't be scared by the equations! Let's break them down piece by piece:

  • Ra equation (firing rate): How fast the population fires depends on: (1) synaptic inputs dampening it (-R * sum of conductances), (2) the product of rate and voltage (2RV, the key nonlinearity), and (3) a constant drive from the spread in neuron properties (Δ/(πτ))
  • Va equation (voltage): The mean voltage evolves with: (1) a background drive (η0), (2) a quadratic self-interaction (V2, from the QIF neuron model), (3) a negative term from firing (-π2τ2R2), (4) chemical synaptic currents (conductance times driving force), and (5) gap junction currents (proportional to voltage difference between populations)
  • g and s equations (synapses): These are "low-pass filters" that smooth the incoming spikes into a gradual conductance change. Think of them as converting sharp electrical pulses into smooth chemical signals.

The model is derived via the Ott-Antonsen ansatz applied to a network of QIF neurons with Lorentzian-distributed background drives (center η0, half-width Δ). The complex Kuramoto order parameter W = πτR + iV fully describes the macroscopic state. The firing rate R and mean voltage V are the real and imaginary parts of W/(πτ). The nonlinearity V2 - π2τ2R2 arises from the conformal mapping between the phase distribution and macroscopic variables. The gap junction terms κvab(Vb - Va) appear naturally in the mean-field reduction (Pietras et al., 2019). The synaptic dynamics use a second-order operator Q = (1 + α-1d/dt)2 to produce an alpha-function response.

References: Forrester et al. (2024), Eqs. 7-9, pp. 6-7 (R, V, g, s equations); Eq. 6, p. 6 (alpha-function synaptic operator Q); Notes, Section 8.9, Eqs. 8.84-8.85, p. 356 (R,V formulation derived from OA reduction); Pietras et al. (2019) cited in Forrester p. 6 (gap junction mean-field derivation)

Network Coupling with Delays

▶
Inter-node coupling (Eq. 10)
Qij gij(t) = kext wij REj(t - Tij)

To connect nodes into a network, we add additional synaptic connections between the excitatory populations of different nodes. Node j's excitatory firing rate REj drives a synaptic conductance gij at node i, but with a time delay Tij = dij/v (distance divided by conduction speed).

This adds extra terms to the RE and VE equations at each node. The total system becomes approximately 12N + 2×(number of connections) delay differential equations. For a fully-connected network of N nodes, this is roughly (12 + 2N)N DDEs — for sparser graphs it is less.

The inter-node coupling term adds ∑j gij(vsynij - VEi) to the VE equation and -REi ∑j gij to the RE equation at node i. The conductances gij are driven by delayed firing rates through the second-order synaptic filter Qij with node-pair-specific synaptic rate αij. Crucially, the delays Tij are heterogeneous - varying across connections based on physical axonal distance - unlike the constant delays in Conti & Van Gorder.

References: Forrester et al. (2024), Eq. 10, p. 8 (inter-node coupling with delays); Eq. 11, p. 8 (structural connectivity matrix wij with row-sum normalisation); p. 9 (conduction velocity v = 12 m/s, distance-dependent delays Tij = dij/v)

The Kuramoto Order Parameter Z

▶

This is a unique feature of the next generation model that Wilson-Cowan simply cannot provide. Z is a complex number that tells you how synchronized the neurons within a population are:

  • |Z| ≈ 1: Neurons are highly synchronized (all firing together)
  • |Z| ≈ 0: Neurons are asynchronous (firing randomly)

The firing rate R is related to Z by: R = Re((1-Z*)/(1+Z*)) / (πτ). So firing rate isn't just an arbitrary number - it's a function of how coherent the population is. This is a profound conceptual advance.

The Kuramoto order parameter Z = (1/P) ∑p eiθp (in the P → ∞ limit) emerges naturally from the Ott-Antonsen reduction. The conformal mapping Z = (1-W*)/(1+W*) maps the right half of the W = πτR + iV complex plane onto the unit disk |Z| ≤ 1. The population firing rate is f(Z) = Re((1-Z*)/(1+Z*)) / π, a nonlinear function of the degree of synchrony. This contrasts with the phenomenological sigmoid of Wilson-Cowan. The dynamics of Z are governed by dZ/dt = F(Z; μ0, Δ) + G(Z, g; vsyn) (Eqs. 8.70-8.72 from Notes).

Section 8.9: The Ott-Antonsen Reduction (The Key Trick)

▶

This is the mathematical magic behind the next generation model. Here's the idea in simple steps:

  1. Start with many neurons: Consider N theta-neurons (or equivalently QIF neurons), each described by a single phase θi
  2. They interact: Each neuron is driven by its own background current μi plus synaptic input from others
  3. Take the infinite limit: Let N → ∞ and describe the population by a density function ρ(θ, t)
  4. The OA ansatz: Assume the density has a special Lorentzian shape parameterized by a single complex number Z (the Kuramoto order parameter)
  5. Average over disorder: The background drives μi are drawn from a Lorentzian distribution (center μ0, width Δ)
  6. Result: You get a single complex ODE for Z, or equivalently two real ODEs for R (firing rate) and V (mean voltage)

The beauty is that this reduction is exact (not an approximation) in the infinite-N limit. You go from infinitely many neurons to just 2 equations per population, with no information loss!

The Ott-Antonsen (OA) reduction proceeds as follows. For a network of θ-neurons (Eq. 8.44) with global coupling via pulse function P(θ) = δ(θ - π), the phase distribution ρ(θ|μ, t) satisfies a continuity equation (Eq. 8.59). The OA ansatz (Eq. 8.64) constrains all Fourier coefficients to be powers of a single complex function a(μ, t): fn(μ, t) = a(μ, t)n, |a| ≤ 1. This yields a complex ODE for a (Eq. 8.65).

The Kuramoto order parameter Z(t) is constructed by integrating over the Lorentzian distribution L(μ) = (Δ/π) / ((μ-μ0)2+Δ2) using contour integration (Eq. 8.67), yielding Z*(t) = a(μ-, t). The firing rate f(Z) = Re((1-Z*)/(1+Z*))/π (Eq. 8.69) is a nonlinear, non-monotone function of |Z|. Via the conformal map W = πτR + iV (where Z = (1-W*)/(1+W*)), the dynamics become (Eqs. 8.84-8.85): R' = Δ/π + R(2V - g), V' = μ0 + V2 - π2R2 + g(vsyn - V).

References: Notes, Section 8.9: pp. 349-351 (theta-neuron model, Eq. 8.44); pp. 351-352 (continuity equation, Eq. 8.59); pp. 352-353 (OA ansatz, Eq. 8.64; complex ODE, Eq. 8.65); p. 353 (Lorentzian distribution, Eq. 8.66; contour integration, Eq. 8.67); pp. 353-354 (firing rate f(Z), Eq. 8.69; Z dynamics, Eqs. 8.70-8.72); pp. 355-356 (conformal map W, Eq. 8.79; R,V equations, Eqs. 8.84-8.85)

QIF Neurons and the Voltage Description

▶

The theta-neuron model can be rewritten in terms of voltage using vi = tan(θi/2), giving the quadratic integrate-and-fire (QIF) neuron:

QIF Neuron (Eq. 8.73)
τ dv/dt = μ + v2 + g(vsyn - v)

This is the simplest spiking neuron model: when v reaches +∞ (threshold), it resets to -∞, producing a "spike." The v2 term is what makes it quadratic. The mean-field reduction of this model gives the (R, V) equations that you'll implement.

The key insight from Montbrio et al. (2015): the probability density of neuron voltages converges to a Lorentzian shape (Eq. 8.76), with center y (related to mean voltage V) and half-width x (related to firing rate R: x = πR). This Lorentzian assumption is what makes the exact reduction possible.

References: Notes, Section 8.9: p. 354 (QIF neuron, Eq. 8.73; equivalence to theta-neuron via v = tan(θ/2)); pp. 354-355 (Lorentzian voltage distribution, Eq. 8.76; Montbrió et al. 2015 derivation); Fig. 8.13, p. 357 (validation: OA reduction vs. QIF spiking network simulation)

The Next Generation Model: Your Implementation Guide

▶

This section brings everything together for what you need to implement. Think of building the model in layers:

  1. Single E-I node (12 ODEs) → validate it oscillates correctly
  2. N coupled nodes without delays (12N ODEs) → validate coupling/synchronization
  3. N coupled nodes with delays (12N + 2×connections DDEs) → the full model

Step 1: Single Node (12 ODEs)

▶

Your state vector for one node has 12 components:

RE RI VE VI gEE gEI gIE gII sEE sEI sIE sII

For each variable, here's what the equation is doing in plain terms:

  • dRE/dt: "The excitatory firing rate goes up when voltage is high (2REVE term), goes down due to all the synaptic conductances dampening it, and has a constant positive drive from neural heterogeneity (ΔE)."
  • dVE/dt: "The excitatory voltage is pushed up by background drive (η0E) and self-interaction (V2), pulled down by firing (the -π2τ2R2 term), and modulated by synaptic currents (chemical: g*(vsyn-V), electrical: κv*(Vother-V))."
  • dg/dt and ds/dt: "The synaptic conductances smoothly track the presynaptic firing rates through a double exponential filter."

Use scipy.integrate.solve_ivp with a stiff solver (e.g., 'Radau' or 'BDF') since the system can be stiff due to the different time scales.

(2024), Eqs. 7-9, pp. 6-7 (single-node equations you implement); Table in S1 Text (parameter values); Notes, Eqs. 8.84-8.85, p. 356 (simplified R,V form without gap junctions for initial validation); Notes, Fig. 8.13, p. 357 (target validation plot: R(t), V(t) should match QIF spiking simulation)

Step 2: Network Without Delays (12N ODEs)

▶

To connect N nodes, you add inter-node synaptic conductances. At each node i, the excitatory population receives input from excitatory populations at other connected nodes j. You need additional gij and sij variables for each connection.

However, for a simpler start (and closer to the Conti & Van Gorder approach), you can use a single global coupling term rather than per-connection synaptic variables. This adds the coupling term directly to the RE and VE equations without extra state variables:

  • VEi equation gets: + kext ∑j wij REj (vsynij - VEi)
  • REi equation gets: - REi kext ∑j wij REj

Start with N=2, verify that two coupled nodes synchronize, then scale to N=16.

References: Forrester et al. (2024), Eq. 10, p. 8 (inter-node coupling terms added to RE and VE); Eq. 11, p. 8 (row-sum normalisation of wij); Conti & Van Gorder (2019), pp. 3-4 (network topology definitions: path, cycle, complete, lattice graphs)

Step 3: Adding Delays (DDEs)

▶

Replace REj(t) with REj(t - Tij) in the coupling terms. Now you need a DDE solver. In Python, jitcdde is recommended - it compiles the equations for speed and handles multiple constant and state-dependent delays.

You'll need to provide a history function (what the state was before time 0). A common choice is to set the history to the steady state values or to a constant initial condition and let the system settle into its natural dynamics.

The full delayed network model follows Eq. (10) of Forrester et al., with the inter-node conductances gij driven by Qijgij(t) = kextwijREj(t - Tij). Here Qij = (1 + αij-1d/dt)2 is the second-order synaptic operator, and Tij = dij/v are distance-dependent delays. The system requires storage of history values and interpolation for non-grid delay times. JiTCDDE uses Hermite interpolation (similar to NFESOLVE's approach) and adaptive stepping.

References: Forrester et al. (2024), Eq. 10, p. 8 (delayed coupling equation); Eq. 6, p. 6 (second-order synaptic operator Q); pp. 10-11 (NFESOLVE solver: C++ with OpenMP, adaptive RK, Hermite interpolation); Conti & Van Gorder (2019), p. 4 (MATLAB DDE23 solver for constant-delay comparison)

Step 4: Functional Connectivity Analysis (Ningqian)

▶

Once you have your network model running, you can compute functional connectivity (FC) from the simulation output. This bridges structure and function: your adjacency matrix W defines structural connectivity (which nodes are wired together), while FC reveals which nodes are functionally co-active (which regions tend to oscillate together). Forrester et al. (2024) use FC analysis extensively (pp. 9-13) to validate their model against empirical MEG data.

Two practical FC methods you should implement:

Method 1: Pearson Correlation FC (simplest)

Compute the Pearson correlation coefficient between RE(t) time series across all pairs of nodes. This gives you an N×N FC matrix directly:

FCij = corr(REi(t), REj(t))

Values near +1 mean two regions oscillate in sync; near 0 means no relationship; near -1 means anti-correlated. This is the most common FC measure in fMRI studies (applied to BOLD signal) and is trivial to compute with numpy.corrcoef().

Pearson correlation applied to model firing rate time series REi(t) produces a symmetric N×N FC matrix. This is analogous to the BOLD FC computation in Forrester et al. (Eq. 17), where the Balloon-Windkessel model converts RE(t) to a simulated BOLD signal before computing correlations. For your project, directly correlating RE(t) is sufficient and avoids the complexity of haemodynamic modelling. Ensure you discard a transient period (e.g., first 1-2 seconds) before computing correlations to avoid initial condition artefacts.

Method 2: Phase-Locking Value (PLV) — unique to the Next Gen Model

This is the strongest novel contribution, because PLV uses the Kuramoto order parameter that only the next generation model provides (impossible with Wilson-Cowan!). At each node, the model gives you Zj(t) = |Zj|eiΘj. You extract the phase Θj(t) from Z and compute:

Phase-Locking Value (Eq. 16, Forrester et al.)
PLVjk = |⟨ei(Θj(t) - Θk(t))⟩t|

PLV measures phase synchrony between two regions:

  • PLV = 1: Perfectly phase-locked (constant phase difference)
  • PLV = 0: No phase relationship (random phase difference)

This gives you an N×N FC matrix capturing synchronisation structure. You can then compare FC matrices across different topologies (Dean's work) and delay conditions (Kavya's work) to see how structure shapes function.

The PLV exploits the complex Kuramoto order parameter Zj(t) available from the Ott-Antonsen reduction. Using Wj = πτRj + iVj and the conformal mapping Zj = (1 - Wj*)/(1 + Wj*), the population phase Θj(t) = arg(Zj(t)) can be extracted at each time step. The PLV (Eq. 16 of Forrester et al.) then quantifies the consistency of phase differences over time. This metric is particularly powerful because it is amplitude-independent and captures oscillatory coherence directly.

Implementation steps:

  • At each time step, compute Wj = πτEREj + iVEj for each node j
  • Map to Zj = (1 - conj(Wj))/(1 + conj(Wj))
  • Extract phase: Θj = arg(Zj)
  • Compute PLVjk = |mean(exp(i(Θj - Θk)))| over time

Forrester et al. also define the GIM (Global Interaction Measure) (Eq. 15) based on cross-spectral density matrices, which captures frequency-specific interactions. The GIM simplifies for single-component signals and can be computed from the power spectra and cross-spectra of RE(t). However, PLV is recommended as the primary metric due to its direct link to the next gen model's Kuramoto order parameter.
