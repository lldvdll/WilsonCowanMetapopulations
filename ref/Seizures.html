Background What is an EEG and how do doctors read it?

An EEG (electroencephalogram) records the brain's electrical activity through electrodes. It captures the combined electrical output of millions of neurons beneath each electrode, producing a time-varying voltage trace — the "brain waves" you see in the simulations below.

What neurologists look for when reading an EEG:

Interictal epileptiform discharges (IEDs): Brief sharp-wave transients that stand out from the background rhythm. These occur between seizures and are the primary diagnostic marker — they indicate where the epileptogenic zone is. On the combined EEG simulation below, you'll see these annotated when they appear.

Seizure onset patterns: When a seizure begins, the doctor sees one of two characteristic patterns: (a) low-voltage fast activity (LVFA) — rapid, low-amplitude oscillations that gradually grow, suggesting a Hopf bifurcation onset; or (b) spike-and-wave discharges (SWDs) — high-amplitude sharp transients repeating at 2–4 Hz, suggesting a saddle-node onset. The pattern informs treatment decisions [5].

High-frequency oscillations (in research settings): With intracranial electrodes, researchers detect pathological HFOs — particularly fast ripples at 250–500 Hz — that are the strongest known biomarker of seizure-generating tissue [12].

Reading the comodulogram: the x-axis is the phase frequency (which slow rhythm's timing you're measuring), the y-axis is the amplitude frequency (which fast rhythm's strength you're measuring). In seizure states, the spot intensifies and extends upward into the HFO range (>80 Hz).

Mapping to the Wilson–Cowan DDEs

(1/Tₑ) dEₙ/dt = −Eₙ(t) + S( cₑₑEₙ(t−τ₁) + cᵢₑIₙ(t−τ₂) + Pₙ + kₙ Σⱼ Wₙⱼ Eⱼ(t−ρₙⱼ) )

ρₙⱼ = inter-node delay → your ρ slider. Controls propagation speed between brain regions.

kₙ = coupling strength → your k slider. Stronger coupling means neighbours are recruited more easily.

Pₙ = excitatory drive → elevated at the node you click (modelling the epileptogenic zone).

W = adjacency matrix → determined by your topology choice.

Your DDE extension: Replace uniform ρ with distance-dependent ρₙⱼ = dₙⱼ/v (graph distance ÷ conduction velocity). This creates a realistic propagation gradient — nearby nodes seize first, distant ones later. On the path graph, this produces a wavefront consistent with clinical observations of ~1–10 mm/s ictal propagation [2,6].
